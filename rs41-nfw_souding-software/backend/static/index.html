02 What it measures

A full atmospheric instrument

Every sensor the RS41 platform offers, read and transmitted with care - and a few the original firmware never used.

Air temperature

The sonde's main temperature sensor, read through the measurement frontend with one- or two-point user calibration and a correction offset for accuracy.

Humidity

A capacitive thin-film humidity sensor, driven with a dedicated heating algorithm and corrected for temperature and sensor non-linearity.

Pressure

A direct readout of the factory Vaisala RPM411 BARO-CAP pressure sensor over the internal expansion connector, for precise pressure at any altitude.

GPS

Intelligent positioning algorithms deliver tracking precision, efficiency and resilience to interference like no other sonde firmware before.

Ozone

Precisely converts the OIF411 electrochemical cell into ozone partial pressure and ppbv, actively compensating for pump efficiency and background current.

Versatile algorithms

Onboard algorithms gather every data stream, keeping the readings precise and the sonde stable from launch to landing.

04 First flight in 8 steps

From bare sonde to launch-ready

A simple path for a typical High-Altitude Balloon flight with precise readings. No prior firmware experience needed.

1

Open the Firmware Builder

Switch to the Firmware Builder tab and pick your board architecture. If you have a choice, the newer model (RSM4x4 / RSM4x5) is strongly suggested - more flash and a better GPS.

2

Set your callsign & modes

Enter your callsign and choose your transmission modes. For a HAB flight we suggest enabling Horus V3 (efficient, weak-signal) and APRS (widely received).

3

Configure the sensor boom

In the sensor-boom section, set the startup temperature the sonde will be powered on at - this calibrates the measuring strip correctly from the first reading.

4

Compile & download

Hit Compile and download the ready .bin. No Arduino IDE, no STM32 core, no variant files - the builder does all of it in the browser.

5

Flash the sonde

Install OpenOCD or STM32CubeProgrammer and flash the binary over the XDATA / SWD pins, following the flashing instructions shown after compiling.

6
Optional

Read the temperature offset

Power the sonde at a known ambient temperature and, in Ground Control, read the correction offset of the main temperature sensor.

7
Optional

Calibrate humidity

Select a humidity range delta in the interface, then breathe firmly onto the humidity sensor (or briefly hold it over boiling water) to find its maximum capacitance range. Note the value down.

8

Re-build with your calibration

Power off, return to the builder, set everything the same, and replace the automatic calibrations with your measured correction offset and humidity range. Re-compile and flash - your sonde is now launch-ready.

Ready to build?

Pick your board, set your callsign, click compile. You'll have a flashable firmware binary in seconds.

⏱️ 2 · TX Timing

GPS-clock synchronised transmission timing. Each mode below has two values:

  • Interval - transmit every N seconds, aligned to the top of the hour. Example: 15 transmits at :00, :15, :30, :45. Set to 0 to disable that mode's scheduled TX. Intervals under ~7 s leave little CPU for sensors and GPS, so test carefully.
  • Offset - extra delay added after each interval slot. Example: interval 15, offset 2 transmits at :02, :17, :32, :47. Handy for separating several sondes flying at once.

🛰️ 5 · Horus Binary V3 Recommended
Enable Horus V3 TX

3rd-generation 4FSK protocol from Project Horus (encoder by Mark Jessop VK5QI). No external payload ID needed - the callsign is in the packet. See github.com/xssfox/horusbinaryv3.

One or more frequencies, comma-separated. The sonde cycles through them all, one per TX window (e.g. 437.6, 434.714 for Poland + most of Europe). Low-altitude fast-TX and the data recorder always use the first one.

Each character adds 6 bits to the packet.

25 mW (level 5) is recommended - Horus V3's 4FSK is extremely sensitive and decodes reliably across a whole flight at low power, saving battery and easing the RF budget. Use 100 mW (level 7) only if you know what you are doing (e.g. very long range with directional receivers).

Extra sensors in packet

Adds humidity-module heater temp, cut-out thermistor temp and GPS power mode (gpspwr) to each packet. False gives the same fields as Horus V2.
gpspwr meaning depends on the board: on RSM4x4 / RSM4x5 it is the NFW Intelligent GPS tier (mode 3) - 1 = weak fix (≤10 sats, continuous), 2 = moderate (11-15 sats), 3 = strong (≥15 sats, cyclic power-save). On RSM4x2 / RSM4x1 it is the GPS power state - 0 = not set, 1 = max-performance / continuous, 2 = power-save.

🌐 7 · APRS
Enable APRS TX

AFSK 1200-baud AX.25 packet radio - the sonde transmits standard position/telemetry packets that any APRS receiver or i-gate can pick up and relay. This is one of the more robust APRS implementations available for the RS41: clean tone generation and correct AX.25 framing mean packets decode reliably on common receivers.

Comma-separated; the sonde cycles through every entry, one per TX window. Use the APRS frequency that is standard for your region. The data recorder and low-altitude fast-TX use the first entry only.

The station callsign the packet is sent from. Shown as the tracked object on the map.

Number appended to the callsign (CALL-11). By convention 11 means a balloon / high-altitude tracker.

Free text added to the end of every packet (visible next to the station on the map).

Icon shown for your station on APRS maps. Balloon = O, weather station = _. Custom lets you type any APRS symbol code.

APRS symbol table the icon is taken from. a is the primary table; leave as-is unless you know you need the alternate (/) table.

AX.25 destination / tocall. APRNFW is the official registered tocall for this firmware - it is recognised and officially supported by APRS and identifies the device as running RS41-NFW. Leave it unchanged.

Relay path requested for the packet. WIDE2 with SSID 1 (next field) is the usual airborne setting.

Hop count for the path above (the "n" in WIDEn-N). 1 is typical for flights.

Mode 1 (tracker) packs telemetry into the comment as: F=frame, S=sats, V=battery(V), C=ascent rate(m/s), I=int. temp, T=ext. temp, H=humidity, P=pressure(hPa), J=jamming warning, R=PCB revision. Mode 2 sends a standard APRS weather report instead.

Unlike Horus, APRS is narrowband AFSK that competes for a shared channel, so it benefits from power: 100 mW (level 7) gives the best range and reliability and is the usual choice. Back off to 50/25 mW only where the APRS network is very dense, to avoid hogging the channel and overloading nearby digipeaters.

💡 11 · Status LEDs
Enable status LEDs

On-board green/red LEDs that show what the sonde is doing at a glance.

m

LEDs switch off above this altitude to save power (and avoid drawing attention to a landed sonde). Set 0 to keep them on the whole flight.

🟢 Normal operation (after boot)
  • Solid green - all OK, GPS fix held, no warnings. Ready / flying normally.
  • Blinking orange - running but no GPS fix yet, or a non-critical warning (searching for satellites, low-ish battery).
  • Blinking / solid red - an error is active (sensor-boom fault, RPM411 fault, calibration error or battery warning). Check Ground Control's log.
  • Off - LEDs disabled, or the sonde climbed above the auto-disable altitude.
🟠 Start-up & calibration phase
  • Solid red - hardware initialisation right after power-on (stages 01-04).
  • Blinking orange - calibration in progress (temperature / reconditioning / zero-humidity). During reconditioning & zero-humidity the module is hot (~140 °C) - keep clear of the boom.
  • Red blinks - an error during start-up or calibration (e.g. sensor-boom fault or calibration error). Check the log.
  • 5 quick green blinks - boot complete, system started and entering normal operation.
🔌 12 · XDATA Port / Instruments
Mode 0Port disabled. Nothing is transmitted or read - use when no instrument is connected and the XDATA port is unused.
Mode 1115200 bps - the standard Ground Control link. Outputs human-readable [info]/[warn]/[err] diagnostics and compact $NFW telemetry frames that Ground Control decodes, and accepts commands back. The everyday choice for bench work, calibration and pre-flight: connect a USB-UART adapter and open Ground Control at 115200 bps.
Mode 2115200 bps. A plain UART pass-through: the raw GPS NMEA stream is echoed to the port. Useful as a standalone UART/NMEA source for other devices (loggers, a PC, another tracker) or for GPS-module debugging. Not a Ground Control link.
Mode 39600 bps - OIF411 ozone + Ground Control together. The firmware reads OIF411 xdata frames and sends $NFW telemetry extended with ozone data (fields 130-146). The XDATA UART is full-duplex, so Ground Control is fully supported the whole time alongside the instrument - just set the ground-station baud rate to 9600 (a one-click change in Ground Control) and you get the live dashboard, the OIF411 card and the full command set, exactly like Mode 1.
🌡️ 12b · OIF411 Ozone Sonde Settings MODE 3 ONLY
t₁₀₀ is the time for the OIF411's internal pump to push 100 ml of air through the electrochemical cell cathode. Measure it with a stopwatch and a calibrated flow meter - no ozone-destruction filter is needed for this measurement. A value of 28.5 s is typical for a well-maintained OIF411; replace with your measured value for best accuracy.
I₀ (background current) is the residual cell current in ozone-free air at ground level. Supply zero-O₃ air using an ozone-destruction filter, run the pump at its nominal rate, and read the stabilised current. The firmware uses I₀ to compute the pressure-dependent background Ibg(P) throughout the flight.
RPM411 is the Vaisala pressure sensor board - not a pump. It connects to the rear connector of the sonde and supplies ambient pressure P, which the firmware uses for Cef interpolation in the ozone partial-pressure calculation.
Data Recorder is strongly recommended - it adds the full raw OIF411 readings (pumpT, I, pumpU, pumpI, extV, diagnostics) alongside the flight data, so you capture the complete ozone picture for later analysis.
s

Time for the OIF411's internal pump to push 100 ml of air through the cathode cell. Measure with a stopwatch and a calibrated flow meter (no ozone-destruction filter needed here). Default 28.5 s is typical for a well-maintained OIF411.

μA

Zero-air background current measured at ground level with an ozone-destruction filter in place. Used to compute Ibg(P) = I₀ × (A₀+A₁P+A₂P²)/(A₀+A₁P₀+A₂P₀²). Default 0.015 μA.

🎯 16 · Temperature Calibration
Why calibrate? Each RS41 sensor boom's PT1000 thermometer has a small individual offset - typically 0-3 °C. Auto-calibration measures and corrects it at every power-on so readings in flight are accurate without hand-calculation.
Method 1 - Known ambient temperature (±0-1.5 °C, most accurate). Enter the precise temperature of the environment where you will power on the sonde (e.g. your room, measured with a calibrated thermometer). The firmware compares the sensor reading to that reference and stores the offset. The sonde must be powered on in that exact environment for the calibration to be valid.
Method 2 - PCB thermal model (±0-3 °C, no reference needed). The firmware averages the onboard PCB temperature sensors and applies a self-heating polynomial correction to estimate air temperature. For this to work the sonde must have acclimatised to the air for at least 30 min before power-on (in the same room or outside, whichever is your launch environment). Accurate when PCB is fully stabilised.
Manual offset (most precise - same accuracy as Method 1). Power the sonde on, wait for readings to stabilise, then hold a calibrated reference thermometer next to the sensor hook. Calculate: mainTemperatureCorrectionC = (reference) - (sonde reading). Valid for this boom only. If the correction exceeds ±25 °C the sensor may be faulty.
Successful calibration is signalled by 3 green LED blinks. After calibration completes you can also read the resulting Temp. Calibration Factor on the Sensors tab and paste it as the manual offset to skip the calibration step in future builds.

💧 17 · Humidity Module
How it works. The firmware heats the humidity module (the small capacitive sensor on the boom) above 100 °C, forcing 0 %RH, and takes a baseline capacitance reading. In flight, RH is derived from the live capacitance relative to that baseline. On top of the basic capacitance-to-RH conversion, the firmware further refines the reading to compensate for sensor-surface heating, the sensor's inherent non-linearity, and its temperature and other cross-dependencies - so the output stays accurate across the wide temperature and humidity range of a flight.
Zero-humidity calibration procedure. Place the sonde indoors in still air, 5-40 °C, 0-60 %RH. Power on with Zero-humidity calibration enabled - the LED blinks orange while it runs. The sensor boom gets very hot (~140 °C) - do not touch it. Calibration takes about 1 minute. Allow the sensor to cool for ~1 min before relying on humidity readings. If it fails repeatedly outdoors, try moving indoors with lower humidity.
Reconditioning. Briefly heats the sensor to ~140 °C to burn off surface moisture and contamination. Run it together with zero-humidity calibration - especially worthwhile if there is any chance the sensor surface has touched something and could have been left with a residue or film that would skew the readings. This is the same procedure Vaisala applies at the launch site.
Capacitance range delta. The capacitance span between the dry baseline (0 %RH) and a fully saturated sensor (100 %RH). Default 6 is an empirical average across multiple booms. For best accuracy, measure your specific sensor with Humidity range debug mode (see below). If your measured value is far outside the expected range, consider replacing the sensor boom.
In-flight humidity module heating. Gently keeps the sensor above air temperature to stop condensation and frost from forming on it. Recommended - it noticeably improves measurement accuracy, especially in difficult conditions (cloud, icing, high humidity), uses very little power, and runs fully autonomously and intelligently. Enabled in Section 19 (Heating).

Humidity range debug mode

A one-time helper for measuring your sensor's exact capacitance range delta (the field above) instead of relying on the default of 6. There are two ways in: enable it here at compile time (the sonde enters it automatically on boot), or - without recompiling - ask for it from Ground Control with the Humidity Range Cal. command. Either way a dedicated window opens in Ground Control; in xdata mode 1 the sonde also prints plain text logs. The window shows the peak delta computed live from the sonde's readings and has a button to end the calibration. How to use it:

  • Power the sonde on and let the zero-humidity (0 %RH) calibration finish first.
  • Expose the humidity sensor to ~100 %RH: hold it above (not touching) boiling water, sensor pointing up so no condensation forms on it.
  • Watch the live peak value in the window settle on its highest stable reading.
  • Once you are confident the value is real, enter it in Capacitance range delta above and rebuild for the highest measurement accuracy - then end the calibration and turn this mode back off for normal flights.

🔥 19 · Heating
Reference area heating

Holds the temperature of the PCB cut-out where the reference resistors and capacitor for the measurement circuits sit. Keeping that reference warm and stable preserves temperature- and humidity-measurement precision throughout the flight. Recommended for ≤18 h flights on 2×AA; disable on 1×AA or with no sensor boom.

°C
  • 18 °C - original Vaisala styrofoam housing (recommended; best factory-grade precision).
  • 8 °C - improvised wind cover (tape / foil).
  • 0 °C - bare PCB, no housing (very power-hungry).

18 °C gives the best measurement accuracy. Lower targets save power but have a noticeable effect on the readings, so choose with the flight's priorities in mind.

Humidity module heating

Gently keeps the humidity sensor above air temperature so condensation and frost cannot form on it, and also holds the sensor's silicon out of the unfavourable temperature range where its response efficiency and precision drop sharply - so you get noticeably better readings in extreme conditions. Recommended: it improves accuracy (cloud, icing, high humidity), draws very little power, and runs fully autonomously and intelligently. Recommended whenever the sensor boom is fitted.

📈 21 · Flight Computing
m

How far the sonde must climb above its launch altitude to be marked as flying. The baseline is captured at the first GPS fix, so detection works at any launch elevation and for any ascent rate (including slow solar / zero-pressure balloons that a fixed-altitude or per-minute test would miss). Liftoff is declared once this climb is held for several consecutive fixes, which rejects GPS noise. Default 150 m.

m

Balloon burst is detected when the altitude has dropped at least this far below the highest altitude reached so far - i.e. the sonde was climbing, then fell back by this margin, which only happens after the balloon bursts and the payload starts descending. Used to arm landing detection and (on RSM4x4) the low-altitude fast-TX recovery mode. Default 800 m.

💾 24 · Data Recorder
Strongly recommended - well worth it. Every interval the Data Recorder sends a short burst of extra Horus V3 packets carrying a rich set of data that normal telemetry doesn't include: GPS HDOP, jamming indicator and reset counter, the full flight envelope (max altitude, max speed, max ascent/descent rates, temperature extremes), heater PWM state, radio power setting and battery voltage - and in XDATA Mode 3, the complete raw OIF411 ozone readings too. It costs only a little extra power and air-time, yet the data it adds is genuinely fascinating and often turns out useful - a great way to see everything NFW and the Horus V3 extraSensors can report.
